Most companies launch AI initiatives with genuine excitement. They hire data scientists, partner with vendors, kick off a proof-of-concept. Six months later, the notebook sits on a server gathering dust.

We've seen this pattern dozens of times. Here's what actually goes wrong — and what it takes to be in the 15% that ships.

## The Problem Isn't the Technology

The most common assumption is that AI projects fail because the models aren't good enough, or the data is too messy, or the infrastructure is too slow. These are real obstacles, but they're rarely the root cause.

The real reason most AI projects fail is simpler: **they solve the wrong problem.**

Teams rush to build before they've defined what success looks like. They choose a use case because it sounds impressive, not because it will move a P&L. They optimize for model accuracy when the business cares about cost reduction.

## The Double Diamond Antidote

The best AI projects we've worked on share one thing: they invested heavily upfront in problem definition before writing a single line of code.

We use the Double Diamond framework — originally developed by the British Design Council — specifically adapted for AI projects. The first diamond forces rigorous problem discovery and definition. Only after that do we enter the second diamond: solution development and delivery.

The Discover phase alone — stakeholder interviews, data audits, process mapping — often surfaces surprises that would have derailed a project built on assumptions.

## Five Failure Patterns We See Every Time

1. **Executive sponsorship without operational ownership.** The C-suite approves the initiative, but nobody on the ground owns the outcome.

2. **Data optimism.** Teams assume their data is ready. It never is. Plan for 40-60% of project time to be data work.

3. **Point solution thinking.** The model works, but it doesn't connect to any actual workflow. Users ignore it.

4. **Precision vs. value confusion.** 99% model accuracy doesn't matter if the model predicts the wrong thing.

5. **No change management.** AI requires people to work differently. Most organizations underinvest here by a factor of 10.

## What the 15% Does Differently

Production AI projects share a common thread: they treat AI as a business initiative, not a technology project. They have a clear owner, a defined business case, and a plan for adoption before the first line of code.

The technology is the easy part. The hard part — and the part that matters — is everything else.
