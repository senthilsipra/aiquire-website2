Every week we get asked the same question: "Should we fine-tune the model or build a RAG system?"

The short answer: it depends on whether your use case is about knowledge or behavior.

## The Core Distinction

**Fine-tuning** changes how a model behaves — its tone, format, reasoning style, domain vocabulary. It bakes patterns into the model's weights.

**RAG (Retrieval-Augmented Generation)** changes what a model knows at inference time — feeding it relevant context from your knowledge base before it generates a response.

These solve different problems. Conflating them leads to expensive mistakes.

## When to Fine-Tune

Fine-tuning makes sense when:
- You need highly consistent output format (e.g., structured JSON extraction)
- You're teaching domain-specific reasoning patterns
- The behavior change is stable (doesn't need frequent updates)
- You have 500+ high-quality labeled examples

Fine-tuning is NOT the right tool when you need the model to answer questions about your latest internal documents. It doesn't memorize facts well — it learns patterns.

## When to Build RAG

RAG makes sense when:
- Your knowledge base changes frequently
- You need to cite sources and show your work
- You're dealing with proprietary information that shouldn't leave your environment
- You want to avoid hallucination on specific factual questions

RAG's weakness: it requires good retrieval. Garbage in, garbage out. If your chunking strategy is poor or your embeddings don't capture semantic similarity well, the model never gets the right context.

## The Hybrid Approach

Many production systems use both: a fine-tuned model (for domain behavior) on top of a RAG pipeline (for knowledge retrieval). This combination delivers both consistency and accuracy.

The key is sequencing: get your RAG pipeline working first. Validate that retrieval quality is high. Then consider fine-tuning if you still have behavior gaps.

## Our Decision Framework

1. Is this about **behavior** (format, tone, reasoning)? → Fine-tune
2. Is this about **knowledge** (specific facts, documents, recent data)? → RAG
3. Is the knowledge **dynamic** (changes weekly/monthly)? → RAG only, skip fine-tune until knowledge is stable
4. Do you have **fewer than 200 examples**? → Don't fine-tune yet; use few-shot prompting instead
5. Do you need to **cite sources**? → RAG required

When in doubt, start with RAG. It's faster to iterate, easier to debug, and more explainable to stakeholders.
